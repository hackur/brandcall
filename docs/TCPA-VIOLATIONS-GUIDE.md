## 1. Overview of TCPA

### The Law
**Telephone Consumer Protection Act of 1991**
- **Statute:** 47 U.S.C. § 227
- **FCC Implementing Rules:** 47 CFR § 64.1200

### Purpose
Enacted to protect consumers from:
- Unwanted telemarketing calls
- Robocalls and autodialers
- Unsolicited faxes
- Caller ID spoofing and fraud

### Key Amendments
| Year | Amendment | What It Added |
|------|-----------|---------------|
| 2003 | Do-Not-Call Implementation Act | National DNC Registry |
| 2009 | FCC Robocall Rules | Prior express consent required |
| 2010 | Truth in Caller ID Act | Caller ID spoofing penalties |
| 2012 | FCC TCPA Order | Written consent for marketing |
| 2015 | FCC Declaratory Ruling | Expanded ATDS definition (later narrowed) |
| 2021 | Facebook v. Duguid | Narrowed ATDS definition |

## 2. Caller ID Violations

### 2.1 Caller ID Blocking (*67)

**Regulation:** 47 U.S.C. § 227(e); 47 CFR § 64.1601(e)

**The Rule:**
> "No person or entity in the United States shall transmit or cause to be transmitted misleading or inaccurate caller identification information with the intent to defraud, cause harm, or wrongfully obtain anything of value."

**What Constitutes a Violation:**
- Using *67 to block caller ID on telemarketing calls
- Displaying "Unknown" or "Private" for commercial calls
- Failing to transmit caller ID information

**Key Interpretation:**
The FCC has ruled that intentionally blocking caller ID on commercial calls can constitute "inaccurate" information under the Truth in Caller ID Act.

**Reference:** FCC Truth in Caller ID Act Rules, 47 CFR § 64.1604

---

### 2.2 Caller ID Spoofing

**Regulation:** Truth in Caller ID Act of 2009; 47 U.S.C. § 227(e)

**The Rule:**
> Prohibits transmitting misleading or inaccurate caller ID information "with the intent to defraud, cause harm, or wrongfully obtain anything of value."

**What Constitutes a Violation:**
- Displaying a number you don't have rights to use
- Displaying a fake business name
- "Neighbor spoofing" (displaying local numbers you don't own)
- Impersonating government agencies or businesses

**Exceptions:**
- Law enforcement (with authorization)
- Blocking caller ID for legitimate privacy reasons (non-commercial)
- Displaying a number you have legal authority to use (e.g., your business's main line)

**Reference:** 
- FCC Report and Order, FCC 11-100 (June 2011)
- 47 CFR § 64.1604

---

### 2.3 CNAM (Caller Name) Manipulation

**Regulation:** 47 CFR § 64.1601(e)

**The Rule:**
Caller name information must be accurate and not misleading.

**What Constitutes a Violation:**
- Displaying a company name you're not affiliated with
- Using misleading names ("IRS," "Social Security," "Your Bank")
- Changing CNAM to avoid spam filtering

**Reference:** FCC Declaratory Ruling, CG Docket No. 02-278

## 3. Robocall & Autodialer Violations

### 3.1 Autodialer (ATDS) Without Consent

**Regulation:** 47 U.S.C. § 227(b)(1)(A)

**The Rule:**
> It is unlawful "to make any call (other than a call made for emergency purposes or made with the prior express consent of the called party) using any automatic telephone dialing system or an artificial or prerecorded voice... to any telephone number assigned to a... cellular telephone service."

**What Is an ATDS (Post-Duguid):**
Per Facebook v. Duguid (2021), an ATDS must have the capacity to:
1. Store numbers using a random or sequential number generator, OR
2. Produce numbers using a random or sequential number generator

**What Constitutes a Violation:**
- Using an autodialer to call cell phones without prior express consent
- Using predictive dialers that dial from randomly generated lists
- Systems that automatically dial stored lists without human intervention (disputed post-Duguid)

**Reference:**
- Facebook, Inc. v. Duguid, 592 U.S. ___ (2021)
- 47 CFR § 64.1200(a)(1)

---

### 3.2 Artificial/Prerecorded Voice Without Consent

**Regulation:** 47 U.S.C. § 227(b)(1)(A)-(B)

**The Rule:**
Calls using artificial or prerecorded voices to cell phones or residential lines require prior express consent (informational) or prior express written consent (marketing).

**What Constitutes a Violation:**
- Robocalls to cell phones without consent
- Prerecorded telemarketing to residential lines without written consent
- AI-generated voices without proper disclosure and consent

**Reference:** 47 CFR § 64.1200(a)(2)-(3)

## 4. Do Not Call Violations

### 4.1 National Do Not Call Registry

**Regulation:** 47 CFR § 64.1200(c)(2)

**The Rule:**
Telemarketers must not call numbers on the National Do Not Call Registry unless:
- They have an established business relationship (within 18 months of transaction), OR
- They have prior express written consent

**What Constitutes a Violation:**
- Calling a number on the DNC list without exemption
- Failing to scrub call lists against the registry every 31 days
- Ignoring DNC requests from consumers

**Registry Access:** https://www.donotcall.gov

**Reference:** 
- 47 CFR § 64.1200(c)(2)
- 16 CFR § 310.4(b)(1)(iii) (FTC Telemarketing Sales Rule)

---

### 4.2 Company-Specific Do Not Call

**Regulation:** 47 CFR § 64.1200(d)

**The Rule:**
Companies must maintain their own internal do-not-call list and honor consumer requests not to be called.

**What Constitutes a Violation:**
- Failing to maintain an internal DNC list
- Calling someone who previously requested not to be called
- Not honoring DNC requests within a reasonable time (immediately for future calls)
- Not maintaining DNC records for 5 years

**Reference:** 47 CFR § 64.1200(d)(1)-(6)

---

### 4.3 Established Business Relationship (EBR) Abuse

**Regulation:** 47 CFR § 64.1200(c)(2)(iv)

**The Rule:**
An EBR allows calls only:
- Within 18 months of a purchase/transaction, OR
- Within 3 months of an inquiry/application

**What Constitutes a Violation:**
- Calling beyond the EBR timeframe
- Misrepresenting an EBR exists
- Continuing to call after consumer ends the EBR

**Reference:** 47 CFR § 64.1200(c)(2)(iv)

---

## 5. Time-of-Day Violations

### 5.1 Calling Outside Permitted Hours

**Regulation:** 47 CFR § 64.1200(c)(1)

**The Rule:**
> Telephone solicitations may not be made to residential telephone subscribers before 8:00 a.m. or after 9:00 p.m. (local time at the called party's location).

**What Constitutes a Violation:**
- Calling before 8:00 AM local time
- Calling after 9:00 PM local time
- Not accounting for the recipient's time zone

**Important Note:** 
Time is determined by the **recipient's** location, not the caller's.

**Reference:** 47 CFR § 64.1200(c)(1)

## 6. Prerecorded Message Violations

### 6.1 Required Disclosures

**Regulation:** 47 CFR § 64.1200(b)

**The Rule:**
All prerecorded/artificial voice messages must include:
1. Identity of the business/individual/entity initiating the call
2. Telephone number of the calling party (during or after the message)

**What Constitutes a Violation:**
- Failing to identify the caller at the beginning of the message
- Not providing a callback number
- Providing a non-working or misleading callback number

**Reference:** 47 CFR § 64.1200(b)(1)-(2)

---

### 6.2 Opt-Out Mechanism

**Regulation:** 47 CFR § 64.1200(b)(3)

**The Rule:**
Prerecorded telemarketing messages must include:
> "An automated, interactive voice- and/or key press-activated opt-out mechanism that enables the called person to make a do-not-call request... The mechanism must be available for use at any time during the message."

**What Constitutes a Violation:**
- No opt-out mechanism in prerecorded message
- Opt-out only available at end of message (must be available throughout)
- Opt-out mechanism that doesn't work
- Failing to process opt-out within a reasonable time

**Reference:** 47 CFR § 64.1200(b)(3)

---

## 7. Fax Violations

### 7.1 Unsolicited Fax Advertising (Junk Fax)

**Regulation:** 47 U.S.C. § 227(b)(1)(C); Junk Fax Prevention Act of 2005

**The Rule:**
> It is unlawful to send unsolicited advertisements to fax machines without prior express permission or an established business relationship.

**What Constitutes a Violation:**
- Sending fax ads without prior express permission
- Sending fax ads outside EBR scope
- Failing to include opt-out notice on fax

**Required Opt-Out Notice:**
Every fax ad must include:
- Clear statement that recipient can opt out
- Domestic telephone and fax number for opt-out requests
- Opt-out must be available 24/7

**Reference:** 47 CFR § 64.1200(a)(4)

## 8. Text Message (SMS) Violations

### 8.1 Text Messages Are "Calls" Under TCPA

**Regulation:** 47 U.S.C. § 227(b)(1)(A); FCC Declaratory Ruling (2015)

**The Rule:**
The FCC has clarified that text messages (SMS/MMS) are "calls" under the TCPA, subject to the same rules as voice calls.

**What Constitutes a Violation:**
- Sending marketing texts without prior express written consent
- Sending texts using an autodialer without consent
- Sending texts to numbers on the DNC list
- Failing to honor opt-out requests (STOP)

**Reference:**
- FCC Declaratory Ruling, CG Docket No. 02-278 (July 2015)
- 47 CFR § 64.1200(a)(1)

---

### 8.2 CTIA Guidelines (Industry Standard)

**Source:** CTIA (Cellular Telecommunications Industry Association)

**Key Requirements:**
- Clear opt-in disclosure before sending
- "STOP" must be honored immediately
- Message frequency disclosure
- "Msg & data rates may apply" disclosure
- Program name in every message

**Note:** Not law, but carriers enforce CTIA guidelines and can block non-compliant senders.

**Reference:** CTIA Messaging Principles and Best Practices

## 9. Consent Violations

### 9.1 Prior Express Consent vs. Prior Express Written Consent

**Regulation:** 47 CFR § 64.1200(a)(2)-(3)

| Consent Type | Required For | How Obtained |
|--------------|--------------|--------------|
| **Prior Express Consent** | Informational/non-marketing calls/texts | Verbal or written; can be implied by providing number |
| **Prior Express Written Consent** | Telemarketing calls/texts using autodialer or prerecorded voice | Written agreement (including e-signature) with clear disclosure |

**Written Consent Requirements:**
1. Clear and conspicuous disclosure
2. Authorizes specific seller to deliver ads
3. Specifies telephone number to receive calls
4. Signature (handwritten or electronic)
5. Cannot be a condition of purchase

**What Constitutes a Violation:**
- Marketing calls/texts without written consent
- Using consent obtained as condition of purchase
- Not retaining consent records

**Reference:** 47 CFR § 64.1200(f)(8)-(9)

---

### 9.2 Consent Revocation

**Regulation:** FCC Declaratory Ruling (2015); Reyes v. Lincoln Automotive (2017)

**The Rule:**
Consumers may revoke consent at any time, through any reasonable means.

**Reasonable Revocation Methods Include:**
- Verbal request during a call
- "STOP" or "UNSUBSCRIBE" via text
- Written request
- Email request
- Online form submission

**What Constitutes a Violation:**
- Ignoring revocation requests
- Requiring specific methods to revoke
- Continuing to call after revocation
- Excessive delay in processing revocation

**Reference:**
- Reyes v. Lincoln Automotive Financial Services, 861 F.3d 51 (2d Cir. 2017)
- FCC Declaratory Ruling, CG Docket No. 02-278 (2015)

## 10. Penalties & Enforcement

### 10.1 Statutory Damages

**Regulation:** 47 U.S.C. § 227(b)(3)

| Violation Type | Penalty Per Call/Text/Fax |
|----------------|---------------------------|
| Standard (negligent) | $500 |
| Willful/Knowing | $1,500 (treble damages) |

**What Makes a Violation "Willful":**
- Knowledge of the rules and disregard
- Repeated violations after being notified
- Intentional conduct (e.g., using *67)
- Failure to implement compliance procedures

---

### 10.2 FCC Enforcement

**Authority:** 47 U.S.C. § 503

**FCC Penalties:**
- Up to $10,000 per violation
- Up to $1,000,000 aggregate for continuing violations
- Equipment seizure

**Recent FCC Actions:**
- 2024: $299.9 million fine against robocall operation
- 2023: $225 million fine for health insurance robocalls
- Multiple cease-and-desist orders

**Reference:** FCC Enforcement Bureau Actions: https://www.fcc.gov/enforcement

---

### 10.3 Private Right of Action

**Regulation:** 47 U.S.C. § 227(b)(3)

**The Rule:**
Individuals may bring private lawsuits for TCPA violations in state court.

**Why This Matters:**
- Class actions can involve millions of calls/texts
- No need to prove actual damages
- Attorneys' fees recoverable
- "Professional plaintiffs" and TCPA litigation firms actively seek violations

**Notable Settlements:**
- Capital One: $75.5 million (2014)
- Caribbean Cruise Line: $76 million (2016)
- Dish Network: $341 million (2017)
- Wells Fargo: $30.7 million (2023)

---

### 10.4 State Attorney General Enforcement

**Regulation:** 47 U.S.C. § 227(g)

**Authority:**
State AGs may bring civil actions on behalf of residents, with penalties of $500-$1,500 per violation plus injunctive relief.

## 11. State-Level Additions

Many states have laws stricter than federal TCPA:

### California (CCPA + CIPA)
- **Cal. Civ. Code § 1770.90** (TCPA equivalent)
- **Cal. Penal Code § 632.7** (recording consent)
- Private right of action + state enforcement

### Florida
- **Fla. Stat. § 501.059** (Florida TCPA)
- Calling hours: 8 AM - 8 PM (stricter than federal)
- $500/$1,500 per violation

### Texas
- **Tex. Bus. & Com. Code § 305** (Texas TCPA)
- Must disclose call is recorded
- $1,000-$10,000 per violation

### New York
- **N.Y. Gen. Bus. Law § 399-z**
- Additional consent requirements
- $500 per violation

### Washington
- **RCW 19.158** (Commercial Telephone Solicitation Act)
- Must file with state before telemarketing
- $1,000-$25,000 per violation

**Reference:** State-by-state analysis available from National Conference of State Legislatures (NCSL)
